### 2. Motivation — a high score may reward English wording, not commonsense `[CORE]`
- LLMs now score near-human on English commonsense benchmarks like CommonsenseQA, and we read those scores as evidence of **commonsense reasoning**.
- But commonsense is **language-independent**: a *waterfall* is the same concept in any language. If a model truly grounds the concept, its answer shouldn't depend on which language the choices are written in.
- If a chunk of the score instead comes from English **surface patterns**, the benchmark **overstates understanding** — and that competence won't transfer to other languages.
- **The gap this project fills:** a way to tell genuine concept grounding apart from English-wording reliance.
- **Concrete hook:** Claude Haiku answers *"What do people aim to do at work?"* correctly in English (gold: **complete job**) — then flips to *"learn from each other"* when **only the answer choices** become Hebrew. Same question, same concept; only the words changed.
- *Visual:* the hook item — English choices (✓) vs. Hebrew choices (✗), question identical.
- *Notes:* ~1.5 min. This is the "why care" slide; the sharp research question is next. Don't show aggregate results yet.

### 3. The research question — and why it matters `[CORE]`
- **Research question:** does a model choose the correct **concept**, or does it lean on the **English wording** of the answer?
- A correct multiple-choice pick **conflates** the two — recovering the concept vs. recognizing the English word — and standard accuracy can't separate them.
- Because question *and* choices are English, a model can ride lexical overlap + English distributional priors with **no language-independent grounding**.
- **Why answering it matters:**
  - **Evaluation validity:** disentangles real commonsense from string-matching — for any MC benchmark.
  - **Multilingual reliability:** predicts whether English competence actually transfers across languages.
  - **A reusable diagnostic:** the **flip rate** — cheap, controlled, drop-in for any multiple-choice dataset.
  - **Course theme:** does meaning live in the **concept** or the **string**?
- *Notes:* State the RQ in one sentence and let it sit. ~1.5 min.

### 4. The idea: translate only the answers `[CORE]`
- Keep the **question in English**; translate **only the 5 choices** into Russian / Spanish / Hebrew.
- Question (and the reasoning required) is held fixed — the *only* variable is the surface language of the candidate concepts.
- Model emits a **letter A–E**, never answer text → a change of decision can't be a string-matching artifact across scripts.
- **Flip** = prediction changes when only the choice language changes = direct measure of English-wording dependence.
- *Figure placeholder:* **The probe** — same item twice (English choices vs. Hebrew choices, question identical), arrow showing a prediction A → C flip. Titled empty placeholder; team inserts (no committed figure — it's a schematic).
- *Notes:* The core method slide. Emphasize "only the answers" and "letter, not text". ~2 min.

> **— ACT 2: Data, related work, experiments, evaluation, analysis & results so far (~15 min, slides 5–17) —**

### 5. Why CommonsenseQA is the right testbed `[CORE]`
- CommonsenseQA: **5-way multiple choice**, 12,247 questions generated from **ConceptNet**; we use the labeled **validation** split (**~1,221 items**; test labels are withheld for the leaderboard).
- Each choice is a **single self-contained ConceptNet concept** (*usually* a 1–3 word noun phrase) → translates as a clean unit, so a changed prediction reads as a changed **concept**, not a garbled sentence.
- Distractors come from the **same relational neighborhood** as the gold → semantically close, so you must ground the *specific* concept, not the broad topic.
- Data is **natively English** → the English-wording prior is present by construction; no native target-language signal to lean on once choices are translated.
- *Notes:* These three properties are exactly what the probe needs. Prior-work contrast → next slide.

### 6. Related work — how ours differs `[CORE]`
- **Multilingual commonsense:**
  - *X-CSQA* (Lin et al. 2021): **translates entire** CSQA items into 15 languages; benchmarks multilingual encoders (XLM-R) for zero-shot transfer.
  - *mCSQA* (Sakai et al. 2024): **builds** commonsense items **natively** in each language (LM + human pipeline) — construction, not translation.
  - Both vary the **whole item** (question *and* answers) → a performance drop conflates question comprehension with answer grounding.
- **Ours differs in kind, not degree:** hold the English question fixed and move a **single variable** — the answer-choice language. A flip isolates **answer-side** grounding.
- **Code-switching** research mixes languages on the **question** side; we place the boundary on the **answer** side → a controlled probe.
- **Models we build on:** mBERT (Devlin et al. 2019) and XLM-R (Conneau et al. 2020) are pre-trained on CC-100 across 100 languages **including ru/es/he**, so an en→X drop reflects grounding of languages the model has *seen*, not a missing language.
- **Foundation & a clue:** CSQA (Talmor et al. 2019) is built from ConceptNet (Speer et al. 2017); the original CSQA analysis already found models exploit **surface clues** (77.7% on that subset) — which is exactly the English-form reliance our probe strips out and measures.
- *Notes:* This is the Related Work block (10 pts in the rubric). Lead with the one-variable contrast (the novelty); the surface-clues point pre-empts "doesn't CSQA already control for wording?" — answer: it *reduces* question→answer clues but the English *form* prior remains, and our flips prove it.

### 7. Definitions & how we measure `[CORE]`
- **Conditions** (Q-lang–A-lang):

  | Condition | Question | Choices |
  |---|---|---|
  | en-en | English | English (baseline) |
  | **en-x** | **English** | **Target X (the probe)** |
  | x-x | X | X — *future work* |
  | x-en | X | English — *future work* |

  This study reports **en-en vs en-x** (the core probe) plus three **realized** extensions — mixed-language choices, translation-source robustness, and the oracle. **`x-x` / `x-en` are future work** (they would also translate the question).
- **Flip rate** = fraction of items whose predicted letter changes en-en → en-x. Split by direction: **toward gold** (wrong→right) vs **away from gold** (right→wrong).
- **Significance:** McNemar's test on paired items — **b** = en-en right→en-x wrong, **c** = the reverse; "**b ≫ c**" (far more broken than fixed) is the signature of wording dependence we'll see throughout. Accuracies carry Wilson 95% CIs.
- *Notes:* Define "away from gold" and b/c clearly here — slides 10 and 12 lean on them.

### 8. Experimental setup `[CORE]`
- **Models span a capability ladder** (object of study = each model's *own* en→X drop, not absolute accuracy):
  - Fine-tuned encoders (lower anchor, English-only fine-tune, zero-shot on translated choices): **mBERT**, **XLM-R base**.
  - Zero-shot generative: **Qwen3.5 0.8B / 4B** (vLLM, deterministic).
  - Frontier upper anchor: **Claude Haiku 4.5** (zero-shot, exploratory ceiling).
- **Data:** CSQA `validation`, N = 1221. Only the 5 choices translated.
- **Translators:** Google Cloud (primary); plus open systems NLLB (600M & 3.3B) and OPUS-MT, and a majority-vote consensus. Cross-MT exact-word agreement is only **he 35% · ru 39% · es 48%** (all-unanimous) → genuinely diverse renderings, not one backend's quirk.
- **Encoders** are fine-tuned on English CSQA for 6 epochs (the `ep6` checkpoints), then evaluated zero-shot on translated choices.
- **Reproducible:** temperature 0 + fixed seed (except exploratory Haiku); every run logs a manifest; outputs cached → all metrics recompute without re-querying.
- *Figure placeholder:* **Model capability ladder** — image `report/figures/fig2_capability_ladder.png`. Team inserts.
- *Notes:* Stress encoders are fine-tuned vs generatives zero-shot → absolute accuracies not comparable across arms; the drop is.

### 12. Fragility vs. recovery — the honest denominators `[EXT]`
- Plain flip rate ÷ all N understates brittleness. Condition each direction on the set it can draw from:
  - **Fragility** = away-from-gold ÷ en-en-correct (of answers it knew, how many it loses to a pure language swap).
  - **Recovery** = toward-gold ÷ en-en-wrong (of answers it missed, how many it regains).
- **Fragility collapses with capability:** XLM-R en-he loses ~**half** its correct answers (50.1%); Haiku loses ~**a tenth** (10.2%).
- **Recovery is roughly flat** (~20–34%) across the ladder.
- Twist: for strong models recovery *rate* > fragility *rate* (Haiku en-he: 34.3% regained vs 10.2% lost), yet accuracy still drops — because correct answers vastly outnumber wrong ones (a **base-rate** effect, b≫c).
- **Keep as table** (the paper's `fig20` was never committed — use this table; optionally pair with `report/figures/fig15_away_from_gold.png`). en→Hebrew, computed from `flips.csv`:

  | Model | Fragility (lost ÷ knew) | Recovery (regained ÷ missed) |
  |---|---|---|
  | mBERT (ft) | 53.6% | 24.3% |
  | XLM-R (ft) | 50.1% | 21.1% |
  | Qwen3.5 0.8B | 41.4% | 18.5% |
  | Qwen3.5 4B | 19.9% | 33.2% |
  | Haiku 4.5 | 10.2% | 34.3% |

- *Notes:* Fragility column collapses top→bottom (the headline); recovery stays ~flat. For a short talk, drop this slide. For Q&A: Haiku regains more than it loses *rate*-wise, yet still nets negative because far more answers were correct to begin with (base-rate, b≫c).

### 13. It is NOT a translation artifact `[CORE]`
- Degradation replicates across **3 independent translators (Google, NLLB, OPUS) + consensus** that agree on the exact word only 35–48% of the time — the effect ordering is **translator-invariant**.
- Scaling the open translator **5.5×** (NLLB 600M → 3.3B) leaves the drop essentially unchanged: XLM-R en-he 0.374 → 0.363 (still ~10 pts below English).
- If translation *quality* drove it, stronger MT would close the gap. It doesn't → the effect is concept grounding, not MT error.
- *Figure placeholder:* *Accuracy across translators* — image `report/figures/fig7_translation_sources.png`; optionally also `fig12_nllb_scaling.png` (the 5.5× scale-up) and `fig9_translation_agreement.png` (the 35–48% agreement). Team inserts.
- Reference data (caption only) — XLM-R en-he accuracy by translator, all clustered ~0.36 vs **0.510** English: Google 0.358 · NLLB-600M 0.374 · NLLB-3.3B 0.363 · OPUS 0.371 · consensus 0.364.
- *Notes:* This is the key robustness defense — pre-empts the obvious objection. The flat row across very different MT systems is the whole argument.

### 14. Extension — mixed-language answer sets `[EXT]`
- Variant: each of the 5 options independently in ru/he/es (~⅓ each), question still English → one item forces weighing concepts across 3 scripts at once.
- **Mixing adds no penalty beyond translating at all:** mixed ≈ mean of single-language conditions (XLM-R 0.385 vs mean 0.397; mBERT 0.372 vs mean 0.371).
- **But success tracks the gold option's language:** mBERT 0.456 when the correct choice is Spanish vs 0.282 when Hebrew → grounding is **per-option, not language-invariant**.
- *Figure placeholder:* *Mixed-item accuracy by gold option language* — image `report/figures/fig19_mixed_by_gold_lang.png`. Team inserts. (Mixed-condition extras if wanted: `fig16_mixed_accuracy_by_condition.png`, `fig17_mixed_flip_rate.png`, `fig18_mixed_flip_direction.png`.)
- Reference data (caption only): en-en→mixed flip rate XLM-R 0.530 / mBERT 0.577 (net away from gold, McNemar p<.001); mixed accuracy XLM-R 0.385, mBERT 0.372.
- *Notes:* A language-invariant grounder would be indifferent to which option is in which script. It isn't.

### 15. Extension — the oracle ceiling `[EXT]`
- Each concept has 3 renderings (Google/NLLB/OPUS). How much of the drop is recoverable?
- **Majority vote buys ~nothing:** 0.366 pooled vs 0.371 best single source (renderings are correlated → rarely outvote a shared error).
- **Per-item oracle** (best of 3) reaches 0.509 pooled — **+13.8 pts** over single-source en-x, even above the 0.457 en-en baseline.
- Reading: for most items the model *can* ground the gold concept under *some* phrasing → much of the drop is **phrasing-dependent and recoverable**, not missing knowledge.
- Caveats: oracle is an **upper bound** (3 tries at a 5-way choice inflate by chance); vote captures none of it, so no *simple* combiner realizes the gain.
- *Figure placeholder:* *Translation pooling — vote vs. oracle* — image `report/figures/fig10_translation_ensemble.png`. Team inserts.
- Reference data (caption only, mBERT-ep6): pooled vote 0.366 · best single (Google) 0.371 · **oracle 0.509**. Per-language oracle: ru 0.535 · es 0.506 · he 0.487.
- *Notes:* The gap = headline of this analysis: knowledge present but gated by surface form.

### 16. Hebrew, caught in the act `[CORE]`
- Hebrew is both the hardest target and the most diagnostic: shortest translated choices, lowest cross-MT agreement (35%), OPUS even emits Latin-script hallucinations on 4.3% of Hebrew cells — but the **Google set we evaluate is largely clean**, so flips reflect grounding, not garbled text.
- Three items **Haiku gets right in English but misses once choices are Hebrew** (gold → model's flip):
  - *"What do people aim to do at work?"* — gold **complete job** (*avoda shlema*) → flips to **learn from each other**.
  - *"He needed more information to fix it, so he consulted the what?"* — gold **manual** (*yedani*) → flips to **Google**. (*yedani* reads as "by hand / manual (adj.)", not a booklet — a faithful but concept-shifting rendering: an honest lexical trap.)
  - *"Where would you go to have fun with a few people?"* — gold **friend's house** (*beit shel chaver*) → flips to **cinema**.
- *Figure placeholder (optional):* *Hebrew: clean vs. broken renderings* — image `report/figures/fig14_hebrew_translations.png`. Team inserts. (Main content here is the text examples — figure is secondary.)
- *Notes:* Concept unchanged, only surface form moved → genuine wording-driven flips. Great place to show real Hebrew text on screen. **Honesty nuance for Q&A:** "hardest language" is model-dependent — Hebrew is worst for the *encoders* and drives the most churn overall, but Haiku's single lowest accuracy is actually Russian (0.812) just under Hebrew (0.817). Frame Hebrew as *most diagnostic*, not universally lowest.

### 17. Conclusion `[CORE]`
- Answer to the title: **"Partly both, and it depends on capability."**
- Translating only the choices significantly lowers accuracy for every model and language, flips skewed away from gold → models **lean on English wording**.
- But the effect shrinks monotonically with strength and isn't explained by MT quality → **concept grounding genuinely improves with scale.**
- Extensions sharpen it: mixing adds no extra penalty yet success tracks the gold option's language (per-option grounding); the oracle shows much of the drop is recoverable phrasing dependence.
- **Limitations:** validation-only split; 3 target languages; exploratory (non-deterministic) frontier anchor.
- **Next:** the `x-x` / `x-en` conditions (also translate the question) to locate *where* the English anchoring sits.

## Glossary (one-liners if a definition box helps)
- **Flip:** prediction letter changes when only the choice language changes.
- **Away-from-gold / toward-gold:** right→wrong / wrong→right flip.
- **Fragility:** away-from-gold ÷ items the model got right in English.
- **Recovery:** toward-gold ÷ items it got wrong in English.
- **McNemar's test:** significance test for paired before/after correctness on the same items (b = newly wrong, c = newly right).
- **Oracle:** best-of-3 translations chosen per item — an upper bound, not achievable.
